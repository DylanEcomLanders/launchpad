The pod model is how we shape teams to deliver. Not a project-by-project flat team, not freelancers picked up ad hoc. A four-person delivery cell that owns its clients, plans its own capacity, and gets sharper at the work because the same people keep doing it.

## Why pods

We didn't always work this way. Pods exist because they solve four things at once:

- **Common goal, leveraged work.** A pod has one set of clients and one set of priorities. People inside it pair, hand off, and cover for each other instead of grinding solo.
- **No context-switching tax.** Splitting design between two designers, dev between two devs, and keeping the Strategist and PM as single-points cuts the mental cost of jumping clients.
- **Capacity planning at the unit level.** We schedule against pod capacity, not "which freelancer is free this week." That's a more honest number and a more reliable promise.
- **Room to skill-stack.** A lean pod gives people the bandwidth to grow into harder work before we add headcount. Pods are how we scale without inflating.

> **Pods are the smallest unit of delivery. Not the person, not the project.**

## Capacity

Each pod has a monthly capacity of **40 points**. That number isn't aspirational. It's the empirical average of what a four-person team was producing before we formalised pods. We don't try to push it past that without adding capacity elsewhere.

Points aren't hours. They're a sizing unit tied to what we ship:

| Points | What it is |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | A tertiary page |
| 3 | A core page |

For the full points reference per page type, see the sizing rules inside Launchpad.

Capacity stays flat regardless of which clients a pod is working with. The exception: **Conversion Engine retainers sit at the highest priority** in any pod they touch. If something has to slip in a given week, it isn't the CE work.

## Cadence

The rhythm of a pod's week is fixed:

- **Monday**: kickoff. New work starts, handoffs happen, the week's plan is locked.
- **Thursday**: delivery. Whatever was committed for that week ships.

Project buckets define how many weeks something takes (a Bucket A is ten working days; B and C scale up). Inside a bucket, design lands one week before dev so the dev has a real handoff to work from.

The only full-engagement format is the **Conversion Engine**, which runs **90 days**. Everything else fits inside the week-to-week bucket flow.

A pod-level **weekly standup** is being introduced. The exact format will live on its own page in Communication, but the principle is: a single sync per week is enough if the rest of the work is genuinely async.

## Client assignment

The PM decides which client lands in which pod. The decision considers two things:

- **Capacity**: does the pod have the points to take the work without slipping existing commitments?
- **Niche fit**: pods get sharper at certain client types over time. The PM knows which pod is strongest at which kind of work.

Clients can move between pods when it makes sense. The two common triggers:

- The client relationship shifts and another pod is a better fit.
- A previously low-priority client gets escalated to main priority (often as a retainer upgrade), and routing them to a pod that's already in flow with similar work is more efficient than rebuilding context in the original pod.

Pods own their clients, but pods aren't possessive about them. The point is delivery, not territory.
