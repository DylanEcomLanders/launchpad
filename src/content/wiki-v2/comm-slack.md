Slack is where most of the work day lives. Same goes for client work. Here's how channels are structured and what we expect from each other inside them.

## Channels

Two patterns.

**Internal team channels:**

- `#team` — general team chat
- `#operations` — leads only, ops decisions
- `#design` — the design discipline
- `#dev` — the dev discipline
- `#client-wins` — celebrate when something lands

**Per-client channels:**

- `#internal-[client]` — pod chats freely and candidly, no client present
- `#external-[client]` — client is in the room; the working channel they see

Clients are only ever in their own external channel. Never in any internal channel and never in another client's external channel.

## Response times

During UK working hours, the default response is **within the hour**. That's the baseline a client should feel and what we expect from each other.

Weekends aren't covered by the wider team. The founders will respond when something genuinely needs an answer, but this is an expectation we set with clients up front so silence isn't a surprise.

## When to DM

DMs are the exception, not the default. Most things belong in a channel so the rest of the pod has the context.

Two cases where a DM makes sense:

- **Internal sensitive stuff** — finance, hiring, a tough conversation between two people.
- **Client upsell** by the PM (or CSM, once that role is in place) — keeping pricing or scope conversations out of the open external channel until they're ready to share.

## Threading and reactions

Threading is preference, not policy. Some people thread everything, some don't. Either is fine; whatever keeps the channel readable.

Emojis are for acknowledgment, not performance. A quick 👀 or ✅ to say "I've seen this, I'll come back" beats either silence or a placeholder reply.
